Common Pet Skin Conditions and Treatments

A Comprehensive Guide for Pet Owners

Last Updated: November 22, 2025 | Reading Time: 15 minutes

Skin conditions are among the most common reasons for veterinary visits in dogs and cats. The skin serves as the body's largest organ, providing critical protective functions including immunity, sensory perception, and prevention of dehydration.

Understanding these conditions and their treatments is essential for maintaining your pet's health and comfort. This comprehensive guide covers the most common pet skin problems, their symptoms, and evidence-based treatment protocols.

Understanding Pet Skin Health

Studies indicate that between 6 and 15 percent of feline patients have at least one skin condition, and many suffer from multiple dermatological issues simultaneously.

Dogs experience similar prevalence rates, making skin problems one of the most frequently encountered health concerns in veterinary practice. Regular grooming provides an excellent opportunity to monitor your pet's skin health and identify potential issues early.

1. Allergic Dermatitis (Atopic Dermatitis)

What It Is

Allergic dermatitis, also called atopic dermatitis or atopy, occurs when a pet's immune system overreacts to environmental allergens such as pollen, mold spores, grass, dust mites, or certain food ingredients.

This condition has a strong genetic component, meaning pets whose parents or grandparents had allergies are predisposed to developing similar issues. It's one of the most common chronic skin conditions affecting both dogs and cats.

Clinical Signs in Dogs

  • Intense itching affecting the face (periocular region), ears, muzzle, neck, forelimbs, armpits (axillae), and groin (inguinal regions)
  • Red, inflamed skin (erythema)
  • Chronic ear infections (otitis externa affects about half of dogs with atopic dermatitis)
  • Hair loss from scratching and rubbing
  • Secondary bacterial or yeast (Malassezia) skin infections
  • Darkened, thickened skin in chronic cases
  • Some dogs may have digestive issues including soft stools or vomiting

Clinical Signs in Cats

  • Primarily affects the head and neck
  • Small crusted bumps (miliary dermatitis)
  • Self-induced hair loss from excessive grooming
  • Eosinophilic granuloma complex lesions (raised ulcers or lesions on the nose or lips)
  • Symptoms typically begin after 1 year of age

Treatment Options for Allergic Dermatitis

Allergen Management

  • Identifying and avoiding allergens when possible through allergy testing (intradermal or serum IgE tests)
  • Allergen-specific immunotherapy (allergy shots) for long-term management
  • For food allergies: elimination diet trials lasting 4-8 weeks with prescription hypoallergenic diets

Symptomatic Treatment

Newer medications:

  • Apoquel (oclacitinib) for inflammation control
  • Cytopoint (lokivetmab) - a monoclonal antibody injection
  • Cyclosporine for immune modulation

Traditional options:

  • Antihistamines for mild cases
  • Corticosteroids for acute flare-ups

Topical therapy:

  • Medicated shampoos to remove allergens and soothe skin
  • Sprays and essential fatty acid supplements

Infection control:

  • Antibiotics or antifungals for secondary infections

Sources: Pet Derm Clinic, RSPCA Pet Insurance, Clinical and Translational Allergy

2. Flea Allergy Dermatitis (FAD)

What It Is

Flea allergy dermatitis is one of the most prevalent skin problems in both dogs and cats. It occurs when pets develop an allergic hypersensitivity reaction to proteins in flea saliva.

Even a single flea bite can trigger severe itching in allergic animals, and the fleas may no longer be present by the time symptoms appear. This makes prevention absolutely critical.

Clinical Signs of Flea Allergy

  • Intense itching, particularly at the tail base, lower back (lumbosacral area), ventral abdomen, and inner thighs
  • Frantic licking, grooming, and chewing
  • Hair loss from self-trauma
  • Red, inflamed skin
  • Hot spots (acute moist dermatitis) may develop
  • Secondary bacterial skin infections
  • Flea dirt (digested blood) may be visible when combing

Treatment Approach

For the allergic pet, nothing less than 100% flea control will keep them symptom-free. Treatment includes:

  • Year-round flea preventatives (topical or oral medications)
  • Treatment of all pets in the household
  • Environmental flea control (treating bedding, carpets, and outdoor areas)
  • Anti-inflammatory medications to relieve itching
  • Antibiotics if secondary infections develop
  • Regular flea combing as a diagnostic and monitoring tool

Sources: RSPCA Pet Insurance, Livingston Veterinary Hospital

3. Bacterial Skin Infections (Pyoderma)

What It Is

Pyoderma literally means "pus in the skin" and refers to bacterial infections affecting the skin. In dogs, superficial bacterial pyoderma is the most common reason for antimicrobial use in veterinary practice.

The primary pathogen is Staphylococcus pseudintermedius, though other bacteria including Staphylococcus schleiferi, Staphylococcus aureus, and occasionally gram-negative bacteria can be involved.

Pyoderma is classified by depth: surface, superficial (affecting epidermis and hair follicles), or deep (involving deeper skin layers).

Clinical Signs

Surface Pyoderma

  • Hot spots (pyotraumatic dermatitis) - rapidly developing moist, painful lesions
  • Skin fold infections (intertrigo) in wrinkled breeds
  • Common in breeds like Bulldogs, Pugs, Shar-Peis, and Spaniels

Superficial Pyoderma

  • Papules (small raised bumps) and pustules (pus-filled bumps)
  • Epidermal collarettes (circular patterns of peeling skin)
  • Hair loss and crusting
  • Variable itching

Deep Pyoderma

  • Painful, draining skin lesions
  • Swelling and crusting
  • Tissue damage requiring longer treatment

Treatment Protocol for Pyoderma

Topical Therapy (Preferred First-Line Treatment)

  • Chlorhexidine-based shampoos (2-4%) used 2-3 times weekly with 10-minute contact time
  • Benzoyl peroxide shampoos
  • Chlorhexidine sprays or mousses for daily application
  • Diluted sodium hypochlorite (bleach) solutions at 0.005-0.05%
  • Topical antibiotics (mupirocin, gentamicin) for localized lesions

Systemic Antibiotic Therapy

Used for widespread, deep, or severe infections:

  • First-tier choices: Clavulanate-amoxicillin (12.5-25 mg/kg every 12 hours), Cephalexin, Clindamycin
  • Duration: Superficial pyoderma - minimum 3-4 weeks or 14 days past clinical resolution; Deep pyoderma - minimum 6 weeks or 21 days past resolution
  • Important note: Penicillin, amoxicillin, and ampicillin alone should be avoided as Staphylococcus pseudintermedius produces beta-lactamase
  • Second-tier antibiotics (fluoroquinolones, third-generation cephalosporins) should only be used based on culture and sensitivity testing due to antimicrobial resistance concerns

Additional Management

  • Identifying and treating underlying causes (allergies, endocrine disease, immune compromise)
  • Regular recheck examinations with cytology to confirm resolution
  • Culture and sensitivity testing for recurrent or resistant infections

Sources: Merck Veterinary Manual, Today's Veterinary Practice, International Society for Companion Animal Infectious Diseases Guidelines

4. Fungal Infections

Dermatophytosis (Ringworm)

What It Is

Despite its name, ringworm is not caused by a worm but by dermatophyte fungi that invade the hair shaft and skin surface.

The most common causative organisms are Microsporum canis (primarily affects cats), Microsporum gypseum (soil-dwelling), and Trichophyton species. Ringworm is much more common in cats than dogs, with Persian cats showing particular predisposition.

The condition is zoonotic, meaning it can be transmitted to humans, though serious complications are rare.

Clinical Signs

  • Circular areas of hair loss with raised, red outer rims (classic "ringworm" appearance)
  • Most commonly affects the face, ears, paws, and tail
  • Scaly, crusty skin in affected areas
  • Broken hairs that are easily plucked
  • Itching ranges from absent to severe
  • In dogs: clearly defined borders between affected and normal skin; inflammatory kerions (resembling abscesses) may develop
  • In cats: more generalized infection, particularly in longhaired breeds; some cats are asymptomatic carriers
  • Kittens under 1 year and immunocompromised animals are most susceptible

Treatment Protocol for Ringworm

Successful treatment requires concurrent topical and systemic therapy:

Topical Therapy (Essential for All Cases)

  • Lime sulfur dips (2-4%): Applied to entire body 1-2 times weekly; highly effective with residual antifungal activity
  • Enilconazole 0.2% rinses
  • Miconazole/chlorhexidine shampoo (2%): Used before dips for enhanced effect
  • Topical creams (miconazole, clotrimazole, terbinafine) for localized lesions
  • Continue treatment until two consecutive negative fungal cultures one week apart

Systemic Therapy

  • Itraconazole (non-compounded): 5-10 mg/kg daily; most effective option
    • Cats: 5 mg/kg once daily, week on/week off schedule
    • Can also use pulse therapy with daily dosing for 28 days, then alternating weeks
  • Terbinafine: 30-40 mg/kg daily in dogs; highly effective alternative
  • Griseofulvin: 50 mg/kg (older option with more side effects; no longer first choice)
  • Ketoconazole: 5 mg/kg every 24 hours (dogs only - toxic to cats)
  • Treatment duration: typically 6-12 weeks until mycological cure confirmed
  • Administer with fatty food for better absorption

Environmental Decontamination

  • Quarantine infected pets to limit contamination
  • Daily vacuuming to remove infective spores
  • Mechanical cleaning more important than disinfection in homes
  • In shelters/catteries: disinfect surfaces twice weekly with 1:10 bleach, accelerated hydrogen peroxide (Rescue), or other antifungal disinfectants
  • Culture environment to map contaminated areas
  • Spores can survive for years, making thorough cleaning essential

Monitoring

  • Fungal cultures or PCR testing every 2-4 weeks
  • Treatment continues until mycological cure (negative cultures)
  • Clinical improvement often precedes mycological cure

Sources: Merck Veterinary Manual, World Association for Veterinary Dermatology Guidelines, Clinician's Brief

  • Ringworm is contagious to humans - owners should wear gloves during treatment
  • Immunocompromised individuals should avoid contact with infected pets
  • Never use lufenuron - studies show it is ineffective against dermatophytes
  • Compounded itraconazole should not be used due to poor bioavailability
  • Some cats appear cured but remain contagious carriers - always confirm with culture

Yeast Dermatitis (Malassezia)

What It Is

Yeast dermatitis is caused by overgrowth of Malassezia fungus, which normally lives on pet skin. It commonly affects dogs (and rarely cats) with underlying conditions such as allergies or compromised immune systems.

Clinical Signs

  • Intense itching
  • Greasy, smelly skin
  • Red, inflamed areas
  • Commonly affects paws, armpits, skin folds, ears, and anal area
  • Black or yellow discharge in ears
  • Thickened, darkened skin in chronic cases

Treatment for Yeast Infections

  • Antifungal shampoos containing ketoconazole or miconazole
  • Topical antifungal creams or sprays
  • Oral antifungal medications for severe or widespread infections
  • Addressing underlying causes (allergies, endocrine disease)
  • Regular bathing to reduce yeast populations

Source: Vedder Mountain Veterinary Hospital

5. Parasitic Skin Conditions

Mange

Two main types of mange affect dogs and cats:

Sarcoptic Mange (Scabies)

Caused by Sarcoptes scabiei mites that burrow just under the skin surface.

  • Symptoms: Extremely intense itching, hair loss, crusted skin, red bumps; highly contagious to other pets and people
  • Treatment: Prescription anti-parasitic medications (selamectin, moxidectin, ivermectin), medicated baths, treatment of all in-contact animals

Demodectic Mange (Red Mange)

Caused by Demodex mites that normally inhabit hair follicles. Disease develops when mite populations overgrow, typically in puppies or immunocompromised dogs.

  • Symptoms: Patchy hair loss, red inflamed skin, usually not very itchy; not contagious
  • Treatment: Often resolves on its own in young dogs; severe cases require anti-parasitic medications and addressing underlying immune issues

Sources: Livingston Veterinary Hospital, Vedder Mountain Veterinary Hospital

6. Hot Spots (Acute Moist Dermatitis)

What It Is

Hot spots are rapidly developing, moist, painful skin lesions that result from intense licking and chewing. They're especially common in dogs during warmer weather and are a sign that something is irritating the pet's skin.

Clinical Signs

  • Red, moist, painful skin lesions
  • Hair loss in affected area
  • Rapid progression (can develop within hours)
  • Often found on hips, thighs, or face
  • Foul odor from bacterial infection

Treatment

  • Clipping and disinfecting the affected area
  • Topical antiseptics (chlorhexidine) or antibiotics
  • Anti-inflammatory medications (often topical or systemic corticosteroids)
  • Pain relief if needed
  • E-collar to prevent further self-trauma
  • Identifying and treating the underlying cause (allergies, flea bites, ear infections, anal gland issues)
  • Usually resolves within 7-14 days with appropriate treatment

Sources: RSPCA Pet Insurance, International Society for Companion Animal Infectious Diseases

üîë Key Takeaways for Pet Owners

  • Early detection matters: Regular grooming allows you to spot skin problems early
  • Don't delay veterinary care: Many skin conditions worsen without treatment and can lead to secondary infections
  • Complete the full treatment course: Even if your pet looks better, finishing medications prevents recurrence and resistance
  • Address underlying causes: Many skin conditions are symptoms of deeper issues like allergies or hormonal imbalances
  • Prevention is key: Year-round parasite prevention and good hygiene practices prevent many common skin problems
  • Follow-up is essential: Recheck examinations ensure infections have truly resolved

When to See Your Veterinarian

Contact your veterinarian if you notice any of the following:

  • Persistent scratching, licking, or biting at the skin
  • Hair loss or bald patches
  • Red, inflamed, or irritated skin
  • Scabs, crusts, or lesions
  • Unusual odors from the skin or ears
  • Changes in skin color or texture
  • Discharge from the skin or ears
  • Any rapid development of skin problems

Proper diagnosis is essential. Your veterinarian will perform a thorough examination and may conduct skin scrapings, cytology, bacterial or fungal cultures, allergy testing, or other diagnostics to determine the exact cause and most effective treatment for your pet's skin condition.
